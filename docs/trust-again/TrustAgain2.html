‌Limited corporations

The limited liability corporation is an innovation of the corporate model that protects investors from losing anything more than their investment. If a limited cor- poration creates liabilities greater than its assets, then becomes insolvent, it goes under owing value to credi- tors. However, the owners of this limited liability corpo- ration are not personally accountable for those debts or damages. Owners and investors can lose everything they have invested in the limited corporation, but beyond that level they are isolated from responsibility for (legit- imate and legal) corporate actions and decisions, even though as owners they bear ultimate responsibility for those decisions, and receive all of the profit these de- cisions may have created.

The limited corporation limits downside risk for in- vestors. Viewed through the lens of human sovereignty, groups of individual humans are ultimately responsible for the actions of every human organization, and allow- ing corporations to create a shield between actions and consequences runs counter to core ecosystem principles. Limiting the liability of humans for their own decisions just means that the liabilities end up somewhere else.

Limited liability corporations prevail across the global

economy because, like fiat money, limited liability is a competitive advantage in an economically empty world. Limited liability corporations artificially decrease risk for investors. This artificially increases the availability of investment capital. In an economically empty world, this increased capital can usually be put to positive eco- nomic use to generate real return. However, in a full world, this artificial altering of natural consequences to generate growth becomes a systemically negative force. When these artificially increased levels of capital are run through the debt-based multipliers of the fiat money system, it forms a potent engine for growth and expansion. This engine powered successful economies for generations, but it becomes uncompetitive in an eco- nomically full world.

The principle of a limited corporation is also funda- mentally at odds with the principle of full cost account- ing. Where real costs exist, they must be borne by those whose decisions created those costs, especially when the decision makers profit or could profit from their deci- sions. The limited corporation incentivizes and enables the externalizing of costs onto other humans and onto the natural world.

What a limited corporation sees as externalized costs are always real costs somewhere. In an economically empty world, even if the costs of these externalities are terrible, the economic system remains stable because the costs are mostly borne outside that system. In an economically empty world, externalities to corporations

often remain external to the whole economy. But in an economically full world, what is an externality to one corporation (and its owners) quickly becomes a cost to another.

A factory may treat toxic sludge as an externality and dump it in the harbour, but to the fisheries and tourism industry, the cost is direct and obvious. In an economi- cally empty world, the easiest solution may be to build the factory somewhere else, but in an economically full world, there is nowhere else to build it. The costs will quickly be borne somewhere, and it’s only fair that they be borne by the owners of the factory who will profit off its activities. If those activities then become unprofit- able, that’s just the free market at work. The bound- aries of sustainability, freedom, and sovereignty trump the right to profit. Such profit exists outside the organic economy.

The incentives of the limited corporation are at odds with the sustainability imperative in important ways, as well as the imperative to respect the rights and interests of others. As sovereign humans, we have to step up and take full responsibility for our actions.
