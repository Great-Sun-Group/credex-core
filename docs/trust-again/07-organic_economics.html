Organic Economics

Building on the foundation

Orienting on the heartbeat of the DCO, credcoin forms the foundation of the organic economy. The concept, and calculated implementation, of one equal share of the value harvested from nature today anchors all values in the ecosystem.

Organic economics orients around the flow of economic value creation, growth and consumption. Organic economics understands this flow to be analogous to, and also intimately bound to, the flow of energy absorption and dissolution across the biosphere.

The credcoin equation is based on the values that flow across these chains, and provides the 1.000 CXX reference point that orients us to a very specific variable that we consciously declare will become our constant, as these real and measurable values fluctuate. The stabilized variable of credcoin always orients us relative to the total economic value of the energy that crosses from the natural world into the organic economy today relative to the number of humans who need to be provided for from this energy.

As values flow up our economic chains from the core right to harvest natural resources through various layers of processing and distribution towards consumption, there is a general shape to these organic processes.

The gift is primal

For the vast majority of the time that humans have had economies powered by division of labour, the primary medium of exchange has been infinite variations of the reciprocal gift. The gift in various forms pervades family structures, tribal culture, and many religious expressions. The gift remains central but mostly unnoticed in modern economies.

The sun of mutual value

Although the gift can be more effective at personal scales, it is the qualities of barter that provide the principles for a scalable medium of exchange. The critical and scalable quality of barter is that value is dependably created on both sides of every transaction. This may or may not be true of reciprocal gifts, but it is always true of a barter transaction at the moment it is agreed to.

At that moment, each of the counterparties parts with something that they value less than what they obtain through the transaction. The very motivation of barter is that one receives something that one values more than what one parts with.

Value is created on both sides of a barter transaction. This principle is the fusion at the heart of the star that powers modern economies of every kind. The sun of the organic economy, like every other exchange-based economy, is this barter-based principle of mutual value creation.

Value exchange through credex

An outstanding credex represents an imbalance in the value delivered vis-a-vis the value received. An outstanding credex is a placeholder for value. In barter, each party immediately receives what they value. A credex, like money, enables counterparties to exchange value across complex chains, where those we purchase from usually are not those we sell to, yet everyone receives exactly what they desire (as expressed through purchases), and provides exactly what they are able to provide (as expressed through sales or income).

Markets are competitive structures, but succeeding in a market requires leveraging cooperative effort across vast numbers of humans. Mutual value creation, like the gift, is fundamentally cooperative in nature. The corporate associations that enable and scale this mutual value creation are based on mutual cooperation, mutual risk, and mutual profit. In a well structured market, those who can leverage the values created by others with the greatest efficiency, while providing more value back to counterparties in every transaction than the counterparty parts with, are those who achieve economic success. This is the kind of success and wealth that we can all celebrate.

The growth impulse of profit

As the energy of the sun enters the biosphere, it is absorbed by life, and life transforms this energy into growth. In organic economics, the accumulation of profit is an outward sign of this growth, just as the accumulation of biomass or energy is an outward sign of the growth impulse provided by the sun.

In organic economics, the diversity of the holders of profit is analogous to the diversity of the biosphere. The greater the diversity, the greater the strength, resilience, and ability to innovate to take advantage of new opportunities.

The accumulation of systemic wealth

A system with diverse layers of living biomass naturally creates excesses. In biology these excesses can be seen (for example) as stores of fat energy, rested muscles, and the comfortable capacity to feed oneself into the indefinite future.

The connection between biological and economic wealth can be clearly seen. Where biological wealth (biomass accumulation and diversity) is scarce, it is generally harder to extract economic value. Where biological wealth is abundant, economic wealth is generally easier to obtain. When the ocean is full of fish, it's easy to make a buck in fisheries. When the oceans are empty, it's often best to invest your dollar elsewhere.

These accumulations of economic wealth are capital, and understanding capital as a social force is critical to understanding organic economics. Capital has specific properties, qualities, and desires that need to be understood independently of the owners of capital. Above all, capital wants to grow.

Life is the sun impulse manifest. Capital is the growth impulse manifest.

Symbiotic investment

In the organic economy, investment of accumulated capital is analogous to biological symbiosis, in which resources and advantages are transferred between counterparties in mutually beneficial ways.

In the organic economy, profit accumulates as capital when a human or corporation creates more wealth and advantage for others than is consumed in the process of creating that wealth. This new capital has eager growth energy, and will often be reinvested in the business in which it was generated.

As that continually reinvested capital brings ever greater returns in successful businesses, it matures through the economic cycle. As the accumulation grows, so does the pressure to diversify it into other ventures in the form of growth and return-generating investments. When capital that has grown in one location of the economy moves via investment to another location, the expectation is that it will generate additional value in that new location, then also return greater value to the location from which it was invested. In this way the growth energy of capital flows and multiplies across the economy, contributing both to the distribution (in the entrepreneur) and centralization (in the investor) of wealth.

The gift is the ultimate expression

As the growth force of capital matures, it stabilizes and gradually ossifies, like an ancient tree in the forest after centuries of life, or like a fruit fly in the orchard after days of life. The dynamism and energy of the growth force naturally loses its momentum through time and recirculation. Sometimes the plant absorbs the sun ray, is consumed, and its power subsequently circulates through and energizes many systems of life across species and geographies. And sometimes the plant dies hours after absorbing that sun ray, and immediately decomposes into the soil.

The eventual destiny of all biological life is death, which closes the circle and disperses nutrients and residual energy for the next cycle of life to feed on. And throughout its entire life, every living thing produces biological material that is waste to the individual biological being, but undergoes the same process of decay as the being itself will in death. Life itself creates and necessitates death and decay. All material that is absorbed by life will eventually be returned to the environment.

The eventual destiny of all economic value is consumption. When value becomes capital, it becomes something that can be invested or gifted first, but the eventual destiny of all economic capital is gift and consumption. This organic dynamic closes the circle and disperses value and residual energy for the next cycle of economic value to grow from.

Markets and gifts

Markets tend towards equilibrium, but gifts open potentials for the extraordinary. In barter exchanges, and the markets that arise from complex barter interactions, the dynamic is to offer as little value to another in exchange for the most possible value to oneself. This self-interested dynamic tends towards equilibrium and the equalization of value exchange. The dynamic of desiring and valuing certain goods and services for oneself over others is what gives rise to the possibility of barter, mutual value exchange, markets, profit, capital, and modern economic growth in the first place. And yet the generous gift can be even more potent than the self-interested market.

The gift has always been the primal form of value exchange, and gifts continue to anchor and nurture modern economies in ways that often go unrecognized. The gift is the decomposition of economic value. The gift leads to the final consumption of value, with no expected direct economic outcome. And yet, the gift is the vehicle for economic rebirth.

The power of gifts

The gift of a scholarship opens the path for the next innovative genius to develop the next innovative solution to save millions of lives. The gift of a meal saves someone who goes on to become a search and rescue hero for countless others. The gift of life, nourishment, and a secure and supported childhood gives the next generation potentials that we can only dream of. The gift of education enables a budding entrepreneur to raise their eyes to global horizons and solve a problem being experienced by millions, amass a fortune, and donate billions to worthy charities. The gift of a pair of sneakers begins a basketball career that inspires millions and generates wealth for many.

What unites these examples is that a gift is given without expectation of return, and yet contains a tremendous potential to generate massively positive impacts, including direct economic impacts, for others across the system. It does this by engaging capacities in places and in ways that transaction-based wealth cannot reach and sustain, for whatever reason.

The gift impulse

Whereas purchase transactions are about the creation of value on both sides, and investment transactions are about the growth of capital, gift transactions are about the release of value without expectation of return. And like the rich soil that enables a rich ecosystem, rich gifts enable rich economies.

The impulse to give is as deeply human as the impulse to consume. The impulse to give can arise with the first accumulations of value. Organic economics recognizes the gift impulse as a social force with its own properties, qualities, and desires that need to be understood independently of specific gifts and givers. Above all, gifts strive to provide value where efficient but self-interested market forces do not meet specific human needs.

Parenting and raising children is a gift. Education is a gift. Culture is a gift. Charity to those in need is a gift. Cleaning trash off a beach is a gift. Supporting a cause is a gift. To give is deeply human, and the gift impulse is a critical component of organic economics. The organic economy celebrates gifts as an expression of the highest economic good, matched only by the creation of the value and capital that enables them.
